30 y.o., Male

Q1: How did you get into music?

Definitely from my dad. Listening to just like old rock and roll. Kind of getting into Kiss, old school kind of stuff. And I started getting into the heavier stuff, you know, style singing. Like Slipknot. Kiss was the childhood… Kiss was definitely one of them. But then also Fall Out Boy. Like Pop Punk kind of got popular. And then I had MCR (My Chemical Romance).

Q2: How did you get into metal music?

I would definitely say Kiss kind of gave me the appreciation for the theatrical bands. And then I found Slipknot. And, well, Linkin Park kind of got me into like screaming. And then I found Slipknot and then I really liked the theatrics. So I definitely credit Slipknot a lot. As the years progressed, I got into more heavier bands like Bring Me to the Horizon or Lamb of God, Miss May I. As far as metalcore, I would credit Miss May I. And I mean, I guess Lamb of God is kind of still alternative metal. But, you know, as far as High Fries, definitely Randy Blythe from Lamb of God. And as far as the lows, Levi Benton from Miss May I.

And which Slipknot album do you say is the album?

I mean, I feel like it's obvious to say the self-titled 1999. Because that kind of just—we're coming out and this is what we are, you know, like "Surfacing," "Spit It Out." My personal favorite is "Liberate." So it's kind of more like, you know, fast-paced, a lot of spitting, rapping, and then also screaming. And just the guitars, man, the drums, the drums. Iowa kind of does the same thing, just harder. So I would say it's definitely between those two, but "Subliminal Verses" is definitely a good one, too. Then they became more like mainstream popular. For "All Hope is Gone," it was still like middle school for me.
So it was like, I love Slipknot still, you know, but then it kind of got a little bit softer, I guess. But that's also around the time that I got into heavier bands. So I say they're definitely a gateway band for metalcore, deathcore. Nu metal was kind of like a gray area for a lot of different sounds. Korn, Limp Bizkit, Linkin Park, Slipknot—all had that same kind of edge, their own thing going on. When you hear Korn, you're like, "oh, this is Jonathan Davis." You hear Corey Taylor, it's, "oh, this is Slipknot." They're doing similar things, but in different ways, and that's what made it special. For me, with my music, I want to be genuine and authentic and new, not just the same old regurgitated metal. I want to do something that stands out.

Q3: What metal genres and subgenres do you listen to, and why do you like them?

There's there's more genres than I know what to do with. I would say, typically, I'm listening to metalcore and deathcore just because that's kind of more my flavor at the moment. But I can always throw on some nu metal or just regular alternative metal with a lot of cleans and stuff. But yeah, lately, definitely more metalcore, deathcore, post-hardcore. Lots of screaming.

Q4: What genres of music do you listen to besides metal?

I like a lot of pop punk. Heavily influenced by Fall Out Boy, Bowling for Soup, Cartel from Atlanta, All American Rejects.
Even kind of more dad rock, like Three Doors Down, you know, back in the day. In that same general area—rock-rap, kind of nu metal sound, like 311. I also like a lot of old school hip hop too, the 2000s era, like 50 Cent, Eminem—it's what I grew up with, so it's really nostalgic. But yeah, I'm a big fan of a bunch of different weird subgenres. My guilty pleasure is really good gospel-style singing: Alicia Keys or Keyshia Cole. Like, Keyshia Cole's "Love"—"never knew what I was missing, but as soon as we start kissing, I knew I found you." It's like 2000s R&B singing, with a good rap beat, lots of singing. Some songs, like Ciara, she's rapping and singing, then you get another rapper coming in. I really like mashups, too.

Q5: Now, let's talk about your musical background, if you don't mind. Do you play any musical instruments?

I have really tried to learn guitar. I think I have a short attention span when it comes to something I can't quite figure out. I keep trying, but I can't quite get it, and I end up giving up and focusing on vocals. That's what I've been doing for the past ten years. In 2015, I worked at a tattoo shop — a buddy needed a bass player for his band (Whitmore). I tried to learn, but realized I need to stick to what I like — vocals. I've always been in choir and singing, so I started doing extreme vocals.

Q6: Do you compose music?

Yes. As far as recent band work: the instrumental is mostly done by the band first. I’m coming in to write lyrics. My process is solitary: I listen, close my eyes, visualize the song, start humming, then plug in words. I try to describe journeys or difficult situations, putting my own or others’ stories into the song, describing how it made me feel. For composition, I haven’t had much hands-on, but I fill in the gaps vocally—try to follow the rhythm, fill in dips with the drums, find my "sweet spot" between drums and guitars. A lot of the writing process is very personal. If I'm going through something, I just want to turn on the track and get it out of my head and into the song. Sometimes I'd rather paint, but being in a band is also art, the kind you visualize and hear at the same time.

Q9: Do you have any formal musical education?

Formal? Not college-level, but my sophomore year I was in honors choir. That's as far as I took it in a school setting.

Q10: Are you currently a member of a band? If so, what is the name of the band and how long have you been part of it?

Yes. My band is called Don't Shoot the Messenger. About three and a half years now.

Q11: Have you been in other bands before? If so, how many and for how long?

I have worked with other bands, helped them write lyrics, been a roadie, but not a full-time member.
The band before Whitmore, I was a roadie. I tried to join another band once, but they already had a vocalist. Funny enough, their guitarist now produces our music—he did Full Sail University.
We've all known each other from the Atlanta music scene and help each other out, building a community. I've been part of the scene about seven years. Lived in La Grange in 2015, worked at a tattoo shop, didn’t know anyone in bands. When I moved back to Thomaston, Georgia, I reconnected with people from school who had bands, and at Georgia Film Academy I met more band guys—like Nate Bray, now our producer.
When COVID hit, the original vocalist of Don't Shoot the Messenger quit because he didn't want to do a show; the band wanted to continue. I auditioned online, but someone else (who knew all the songs) got the spot. Later they called me again when he left, and this time I learned all the material and got the job. Been in the band three years since.

Q12: How often do you perform live?

We performed in Macon last Saturday at JBA, as headliners. Last year we headlined a country bar in our hometown. Last Saturday was a multi-band bar headliner. We’re playing again this Saturday at Furnace 41.
We try to do one show a month. We all have full-time jobs and want to avoid playing the same venue many times a year, so we play in different cities—Atlanta, Macon, Columbus, Augusta, etc. It's pretty eventful.

Q13: How long have you been active as a singer or extreme vocalist?

I would say 10 years, since 2015.

Q14: How did you learn extreme vocal techniques?

I would say I'm slightly self-taught. When I was young, I tried to sing along and understood growls and less harsh vocals. In 2015, my buddy Alan let me join a studio session, his band reassured me that I'm good at vocals and should be in a band—so that's when I really pushed further. But even in 8th–9th grade (2008ish) I tried to scream (and hurt my voice). Even my choir teacher didn't know about extreme vocals, so I figured it out myself—at least until gutturals, which Alan taught me. I've also done voice impressions, which helped—trying to sound like Corey Taylor, Randy Blythe, etc. For the last 4–5 years I've been trying to find my own voice.

Q15: Have you taken any vocal lessons? If so, did these lessons cover extreme techniques?

Yeah—just from a friend of mine, who learned from another friend, and they both watched a lot of Melissa Cross. (She’s like the zen master of extreme vocals.) So I watched a lot of YouTube, mainly Melissa Cross, and my buddy Alan (the guitar player for Necrofear) taught me as well.

Q16: Do you follow any specific vocal exercises or routines to maintain your voice?

One thing: when I'm at work and talking all day, my voice gets warm. After work, driving in traffic, it's at full potential because I've been using my voice. But when I first wake up, it's harder. I used to warm up and practice before work. I'd use old choir warmups, like do-re-mi-fa-so-la-ti-da and “rem-sam-sam" stuff from choir.

Q17: What techniques do you use to protect your voice from strain or damage?

Number one: drink water. Stay hydrated—not just the day of, but the day before a show. If you wait until the show day it’s too late, especially if you drink beer. If I’m not feeling well, maybe throat spray helps (but don’t recommend overusing). If you’re sick it’s going to hurt a bit, even if technique is good. One time I played a show at the Masquerade with a cold, used cold medicine and throat spray, did my best and it went pretty good. No regular vocal health issues—only problem was once I woke up with a swollen uvula from sleeping with a fan blowing on me (took antibiotics and two weeks to recover). Stay hydrated so your throat doesn’t dry out.

Q18: How would you describe your vocal style?

I would say I have a late 90s fusion with mid-2010s fusion vocal. When I was in school, I didn’t really listen to metal, thought it was just noise. "Bring Me the Horizon" before Sempiternal was more post-hardcore, inaudible screams. Then clean singing after the vocalist tore his cords. I’d say my style is that 2013 era mixed with 1999 nu metal—Slipknot, System of a Down, Korn. So I was stuck in nu metal, and only went back and revisited Parkway Drive, August Burns Red, and others later.

Q19: Which bands or vocalists have influenced your vocal style?

Corey Taylor, Randy Blythe, Chester Bennington, Levi Benton. Levi Benton stood out because you can articulate his screams—you can actually hear the words, not just the screaming. That’s something I picked up on, and what I strive for.

Q20: Which vocal techniques do you use most frequently?

When I want to enunciate a point, high fry is good. I use low/growls and high fries. Some people say my lows sound more like mids, since I’m a baritone not a bass (maybe I don’t open my throat enough?). So: mid growl and high fries—low lows are not my strong point yet.

Q21: Do you notice any differences in your vocal approach between live performances and studio recordings?

Yes. On stage, it’s easy to snap into the moment because the pressure is on, you get only one chance. In the studio, it’s just me and my guitarist, so it’s more relaxed, mellow—I have to "flip the switch" and get into the energy, get angry. Sometimes I draw on anger from years ago, or use a rough week at work as fuel. As a kid I had anger issues—maybe why I was drawn to screaming. Music became an outlet. Instead of yelling at parents, I could scream along with the music and practice. I would listen to whole albums, learning lyrics and trying to match the singer.

Q22: Have you recorded extreme vocals in a professional studio before? If so, what was the experience like?

Yes. I like it a lot—feels like being a rock star! My first studio recording was for my audition for Don’t Shoot the Messenger in 2019. I never thought I’d be in a studio recording vocals for an audition or a song. Out of everyone who auditioned, it came down to me and one other guy—the other knew more songs and got the spot at first (show was soon). A couple years later, when he left, the band called me. I learned the rest of the songs in a week and reauditioned—this time I got in. That gave me a newfound respect, because I had to earn my place in the band.

Q23: Is intelligibility an important goal for you as a vocalist?
Yes. Many bands don’t really have a message—they just want to sound cool, regurgitating "I hate the devil, the world sucks." That doesn’t have as much substance as a song with a real message—maybe, "this happened to me, you’re not alone." Music can be uplifting, not just negative. I want my lyrics to have substance and a message—especially since the band's called Don’t Shoot the Messenger. I have to have messages in my songs!

Q24: How do you make your lyrics intelligible while using extreme vocal techniques?

I try to articulate my screams so that you can hear the words, not just the sound. For me, Levi Benton (Miss May I) is a model—he articulates so you can actually hear what’s being said, not just noise.

Q25: In your opinion, why do some people (especially non-metal listeners) find extreme vocals difficult to understand?

They may perceive yelling and screaming as negative—maybe from their upbringing, if there was a lot of yelling at home. For some it just triggers bad feelings. Another thing is: they just can't understand it. It's almost its own language. If they're not used to hearing it, they won't give it a chance, or listen long enough to decipher the language. Sometimes you need to read the lyrics to understand. Even Deathcore band logos are hard to read at first—but once you know what it is, you recognize it. Same with vocals: once you know the line, you always hear it. Some lyrics just get branded into your brain.

Q26: How do you think about pitch and "melody" in extreme vocals?

There's got to be a melody in there somewhere, you just have to find it. Example: Ulf Sulfur has a deathcore song "Earthen"—first deathcore vocals, then clean singing. Some people don’t like cleans in metal, but you can find a melody in any symphonic breakdown—sometimes you could add strings for more melody.
