23 y.o., Male

Q1: How did you get into music?

I listened to a ton of like metal, of course, hardcore, a lot of like deathcore stuff, metalcore stuff. I got into it originally—I was raised in the scene. I started listening to Under Oath when I was six because my cousin got me into Under Oath, and then I got into bands like Silverstein, Devil Wears Prada, Bring Me The Horizon, all those, like, the old MySpace deathcore stuff. So I originally got into that when I was younger, kept on going into the scene, looking deeper and deeper and deeper, and I just kind of deep dove. Of course, everybody around me listened to Linkin Park, System of a Down, so learning about those bands and then going from there, listening to more core, heavy stuff with breakdowns—ones and zeros, just stuff like that. So mainly, the MySpace metalcore scene was what I was in.

Q2: How did you get into metal? What inspired you?

I was immediately kind of thrown into everything. So I started right straight from metal.

Q3: What metal genres and subgenres do you listen to, and why do you like them?

Right now, I listen to a lot of metalcore, a lot of deathcore, a lot of slam, a good bit of death metal. But yeah, mainly hardcore stuff like that. Anything with the core element—of course, breakdowns, a lot of melodic stuff, so melodic metalcore, post hardcore, just stuff like that. I just like the intensity, I like the aggression of it. I like how you can kind of throw your body around to it and, you know, at the same time, some of it can be very pretty. You wouldn't expect somebody screaming over a very melodic guitar riff to be comforting, but it's very calming. I've just found a love for it at a very early age.

Q4: What genres of music do you listen to besides metal?

Oh yeah, I listen to a good bit of bluegrass music, some rap—not a ton, and whenever I do it's like old stuff like Nas, Wu-Tang, stuff like that. That's where I've gotten a good bit of cadences and stuff from rap. But bluegrass music, that's what I was born and raised on. Country music, Aretha Franklin—one of my favorite artists of all time, so R&B stuff like that. So it's not only metal in my playlist, but it's definitely there.

Q5: Do you play any musical instruments?

So I don't. I've tried—I've tried time and time again to learn guitar, drums, all that. I can never learn. I'm pretty good with my hands and stuff, I can kind of play piano here and there but it's not good. So I kind of deep dived into singing and screaming at a very early age.

Q6: Do you compose music?

I personally only do writing for lyrics and stuff like that, and cadences. I help people out here and there with cadences and flows and lyric writing and stuff like that. I do write poetry as well, so that kind of ties into it.

Q7: Do you have any formal musical education?

So I was trained by my choir teacher—she does private lessons and stuff like that—when I was younger, all the way through high school, for classical singing. I also did lessons online with a guy who runs the Extreme Vocal Institute (David). He really helped me hone in on screaming, going back, relearning my basics, learning the anatomy of everything—just to figure out what I'm doing without hurting myself. For a little while, I would get on stage, do a 20-minute set, and I couldn’t talk afterwards. That's not good! So I learned how to do it properly—after that I was A-okay, I can usually go about one to two hours without needing a break, no discomfort or anything like that.

Q8: Are you currently a member of a band? If so, what is the name of the band and how long have you been part of it?

Yes, we are on hiatus right now, but I'm a part of a band called Harbinger's Descent from Atlanta. My wife and I had a child so I took a break for a bit, but we're jumping back in. We have a couple of studio projects in the works—me and our guitarist Alex McCrary are working on stuff online. It's kind of like a project where just a couple of people write and then have touring members. Going on three years—that was my first real project. I had filled in here and there before, but nothing notable. This one started up in 2021, 2022. We've gone from there and it's been a lot of fun.

Q9: Have you been in other bands before? If so, how many and for how long?

A couple of fill-ins here and there, but Harbinger's Descent is my first real, notable project.

Q10: How often do you perform live?

We were doing about two shows a month for a good while. We've played, of course, Culture Shock, Masquerade (some of the biggest venues for us), Blackstrap in Gainesville (our first start), and a good bit around Georgia. We were branching out—had shows booked in South Carolina and North Carolina, but sometimes promoters dropped out and those got cancelled. So mainly Georgia shows—we eventually were going to go touring.

Q11: How long have you been active as a singer or extreme vocalist?

I picked up learning extreme metal and doing harsh vocals at 13, so I've been doing it for about 10 years now. It started off me kind of dabbling, trying to learn how to do it. I would blow my voice out, then slowly pick it up and learn.

Q12: How did you learn extreme vocal techniques?

See above (“education”)—trained with classical choir teacher, and later with Extreme Vocal Institute online (David), who helped me understand the anatomy, technique, and how not to hurt myself.

Q13: Have you taken any vocal lessons? If so, did these lessons cover extreme techniques?

Yes—as above, both in person and online. Particularly the online “Extreme Vocal Institute” helped me with extreme techniques, going back to basics and learning proper technique, not hurting myself. After learning properly, I could perform much longer and with no issues.

Q14: Do you follow any specific vocal exercises or routines to maintain your voice?

I have a five-minute warm up routine that I do. On days I’m doing vocals (or lots of vocals), I’ll hum, whistle, and do different things to warm up my voice. I stray away from acidic foods/drinks because they can cause issues with my voice. Eating a lot of food before vocals is a huge “don’t”—it can really throw your voice off for the day. Making sure your voice is comfortable before you sing is critical—if it doesn't feel ready, don't strain or push yourself; but if it’s good, let it fly.

Q15: What techniques do you use to protect your voice from strain or damage?

Avoid acidic foods and drinks, avoid overeating before vocals, always warm up. Make sure my voice feels comfortable before going into a session, never force it if it doesn’t feel ready. If done right, you won’t hurt yourself—I’ve had medical checks and screenings, and my vocal folds look great.

Q16: Have you ever experienced vocal health problems due to extreme singing? If so, please describe.

No. I got checked multiple times (camera through the nose, etc.). Once I lost my voice for a week but it wasn’t due to vocals, just getting sick. For a while I would get on stage and lose my voice for a bit after a 20-minute set, but after learning proper technique, that stopped.

Q17: How would you describe your vocal style?

I like to do vocal acrobatics a lot—jumping around, switching into different styles throughout songs. I use anywhere from about 10 techniques, just trying to figure out what the song needs, not just what I want to do. Learning everything about: "what style are we going for?" (not: "what do I want to do?"). It’s a project-based approach—sometimes a song doesn’t call for highs or lows, just mids or straight gutturals, etc. I try to go with what the song needs. That said, I stick to deathcore stuff: a lot of highs, a lot of lows, midrange, being able to use everything I have and not just one thing.

Q18: Which bands or vocalists have influenced your vocal style?

Tom Barber (Chelsea Grin, Darko US) has influenced me a lot; Alex Koehler (original Chelsea Grin vocalist, now AMY ONA), big influence especially on my highs; Mitch Lucker (Suicide Silence)—amazing vocally and his stage presence was incredible, menacing; Dickie Allen (Infant Annihilator), I’ve tried to learn his “goblin” techniques and nuances; Will Ramos (Lorna Shore), new generation—pushed vocal acrobatics to a new level, especially in "To The Hellfire"; Also bands like Body Snatcher and lots more—I've listened to a ton, pulling little elements from many over the years.

Q19: Which vocal techniques do you use most frequently?

I try to use about 10 different techniques in my arsenal, across highs, lows, mids, straight gutturals, and more. It always depends on what the song/project needs, not just what I feel like doing. I love to use everything I can, not just stick to one thing.

Q20: Do you notice any differences in your vocal approach between live performances and studio recordings?

Absolutely. The adrenaline you get from a live session drastically changes how you do everything. On stage you feel like you can do anything—adrenaline is pumping, you feel nothing matters, but if you rush straight into it, you might blow your voice out. So I slow myself down before going onstage to make sure I don't overdo it. In the studio, you’re in a controlled environment, you can take breaks as needed, tweak little things, really hear yourself. Live, especially if you don’t have in-ears, you can sometimes over-project and not sound technically correct. I try to keep a middle ground—stay energetic, but make sure my voice and technique are solid and I don't burn out. In the studio, I can do more because you can have unlimited takes.

Q21: Have you recorded extreme vocals in a professional studio before? If so, what was the experience like?

(Indirectly referenced above) Yes, experience is different—studio is controlled, live is high-adrenaline, each has its challenges.

Q22: Is intelligibility an important goal for you as a vocalist?

I love slam vocals, death metal vocals where it's just raw, heavy, you can’t really understand it and it’s in your face. But because I write poetry and lyrics, I try to make sure that my vocals can be heard and the words picked apart. Sometimes, just for the brutality, I make noise, but I value meaning—lyrics should have heart and soul, translating into the song. For me, people being able to understand what I'm doing is important. I think it takes more skill to make vocals intelligible—everyone says, "oh, that's just noise," but technique and understanding vowel sounds is key to being understood.

Q23: How do you make your lyrics intelligible while using extreme vocal techniques?

I pay attention to vowel sounds and technique to make sure what I’m saying comes through, not just noise.

Q24: In your opinion, why do some people (especially non-metal listeners) find extreme vocals difficult to understand?

I think it takes a certain ear. I never bash anyone who says "that's crap, that's garbage, I can't understand it"—sometimes people hear it as just screaming, not as pushing emotion through music. A lot of people are raised to believe aggression is wrong, not a “proper” emotion. But you can scream through sadness, pain, being happy, being angry. I never fault anyone for not being into metal—it’s like having someone yell in your ear for an hour can get crazy.

Q24a: Why do you think some people just don't like metal music?

It’s too loud, obnoxious for them—just like pop music is for me, I don’t like it because it sounds repetitive, mundane, simple, all the same. Metal, to me, has so many ways to perform the art, but for others it’s just loud, obnoxious, or they just don’t have the ear for it.

Q25: How do you think about pitch and "melody" in extreme vocals?

Absolutely, there’s melody in screaming and growling. There’s not necessarily a pitch to it, but you can do pitched screams. A scream has a tone, not really a note—a certain airflow you’re pushing out, not necessarily to hit a pitch or note. You can do octaves, not always notes and pitches, but definitely there’s tonality.

Q26: Can extreme vocals effectively convey melody?

Within songs, melody is emotion—the way you ride through the song, feel it. Screaming and extreme vocals can convey that very well. You can put them over sad, heavy, destructive songs, but always make a vibrant or even pretty sound from screaming.

Q27: How do you think about rhythm in extreme vocals?

Absolutely—especially if you know your time signatures. I’ve worked a lot with a drummer in progressive bands (odd time signatures). If you can sync up, use different cadences and flows, you can use vocals to bounce around the rhythms, not just follow a basic 4:4.
